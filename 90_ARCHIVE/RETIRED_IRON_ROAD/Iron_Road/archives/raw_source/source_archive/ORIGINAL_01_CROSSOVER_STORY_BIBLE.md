# THE IRON ROAD - Crossover Story Bible

### The full design, in the spirit of a Dimension 20 season

> *"A road is a promise. It says: you can leave here, and arrive there, and not be eaten on the way. Somebody decided, a long time ago, that the promise was worth more if it could be quietly broken for the right people."*

-----

## PART ONE - THE ENGINE

### Why this crossover works (the emotional physics)

Brennan Lee Mulligan builds seasons around a single principle: **the external threat is a machine for pressing on the exact wound the protagonist already carries.** The villain doesn't just want power - they want to prove that the thing your hero values most was always fragile. You design the world as a pressure system and the characters are the valves.

You already have two protagonists whose wounds are mirror images:

**Brannok Ashhelm - the wound of waiting.**
He saw the signs in the mine. He knew the supports were bad and the safety records were forged. He waited too long to stand up to the men above him, and people died. His entire moral identity since then is *refusing to wait again*. He simplifies moral fog into action: who is hurt, who is hurting them, how do we stop it. His terror is a harm so large and so well-hidden that no amount of action is ever fast enough.

**Selene Vaer - the wound of erasure.**
She survived being hunted by becoming other people, wearing borrowed faces and names. People died trying to help her. Her response is *witness and consequence* - she refuses to let people be made not to count, refuses to be owned or rewritten. Her terror is a world engineered so that disappearing a person leaves no mark, no record, no one who remembers.

These two are perfect foils. Brannok is a hammer; Selene is a mirror. He breaks things open; she makes powerful people see themselves. Put them in the same room and they irritate each other into being better.

### The villain's genius

Here is the Brennan move that makes the whole thing sing:

**The corruption Brannok has been fighting and the corruption Selene has been fighting are the same corruption, run by the same people, for forty years.**

The road-ward network Brannok keeps repairing - damaged wards make trade roads unsafe; repaired wards keep out raiders and beasts - is the *physical infrastructure* of the witness-erasure pipeline the Daughters keep dismantling. The "quiet care" transfers that move Selene's victims into tunnels and safehouses use the same routing system, the same forged safety papers, the same ward-iron logistics, the same "this road is closed for repair, use the other one" authority that Carrow and Wynth were exploiting in Stonewake.

Neither protagonist can see the whole shape of it from their own end. Brannok sees money and broken roads. Selene sees cruelty and disappeared people. The truth is that **money and cruelty are the same operation**, and it requires both a hammer and a mirror to break.

This is the thesis of the season: *systemic evil hides by being too large for any one kind of hero to perceive. It survives because the person fighting the symptom never meets the person fighting the disease.*

## PART TWO - THE THREE-ACT STRUCTURE

The crossover runs as a three-act season. Each act has a fantasy-dial setting, a tonal touchstone, and a hard escalation gate the players must pass through. The dial turns slowly. That slowness is the whole point of Brannok's request: he wants to *feel* the world get bigger.

-----

### ACT ONE - THE MEETING OF ROADS

**Fantasy dial: LOW.** Grounded crime drama. The weirdest thing in the act is a ward-stone behaving slightly wrong.
**Tonal touchstone:** the investigation half of *Fantasy High* meets *The Wire* - two competent people working the same case from opposite ends, not yet trusting each other.

#### The premise of Act One

Brannok and Selene are pursuing the same lead without knowing it. They should *initially distrust or underestimate each other*. The act ends when they realize they are chasing the same routing cipher from opposite directions - Brannok from the money/road end, Selene from the people/erasure end.

#### Key scenes

**Scene 1 - The Ward-Gate at Mirren's Crossing.**
A trade-road bridge that sits at the boundary between Brannok's region and Selene's has been sabotaged: ward-stones smashed, road markers painted over, a cargo manifest removed. Brannok arrives on an official road-assessment errand (a natural follow-on from his Stonewake field-mission options). The Daughters arrive separately - one of their rescued survivors named this crossing as a transfer point.

The bridge is also a fresh crime scene. A courier lies dead - not a traveler, a *road courier*, carrying Survey documents and a sealed message tube stamped with a cipher neither party recognizes.

Both parties want the tube. **Let the players negotiate it.** Do not force cooperation. Let it grow out of the obvious fact that they want the same thing.

> **The first impression, both ways.** Brannok's read on Selene: she's *a lot*, the warlock business unsettles him, but she clearly cares about the dead man at their feet and she has evidence he doesn't. Selene's read on Brannok: blunt in a way she's been burned into reading as honest; he doesn't pretend to be more than he is; he's been in her fight from another door.

**Scene 2 - The Map Room.**
A Road Survey substation attached to a coaching inn holds old route maps with the same cipher marked along trade roads through *both* regions. Someone has maintained these maps carefully. A back room has been used recently; the hearth is still warm. The innkeeper, **Doss Merren**, knows more than she admits - but she's a frightened bystander, not a villain. She's been passing documents to a courier network believing they were "mercy welfare transports." She is the human face of how ordinary people get folded into atrocity by being told a comfortable lie.

**Scene 3 - Rook Finds the Badge.**
Rook (Brannok's crow) turns up a dropped brooch in the stable yard: a replica of a retired Road Survey Commissioner's rank badge, modified - its back filed down to seat a hidden seal. Zula or Eya can identify the seal's properties: it's built to *override ward-stone authentication*, i.e., to authorize false road-safety certifications. This is Selene's world (magical forensics) entering Brannok's world (road bureaucracy). Decoding it takes a wizard's analysis **and** a road-warden's context. Neither alone is enough. That's deliberate.

#### Act One climax

The cipher is partly cracked. It names a central routing hub: a location marked on pre-reform maps as **Continental Survey Station Seven**, administratively deleted from the register forty years ago - but the physical site still stands, and a wagon left for it within the last twelve hours.

**Decision point:** Do Brannok and Selene trust each other enough to go together

### ACT TWO - THE WEIGHT OF SAFE ROADS

**Fantasy dial: RISING.** This is where the genre reveals it has more teeth than Brannok's campaign ever showed.
**Tonal touchstone:** the turn in *A Crown of Candy* when a political drama becomes something darker and more dangerous - and the body-horror edges of *Neverafter*.

#### The premise of Act Two

The heroes work together now, methods and values colliding *productively*. Act Two introduces the first genuinely high-fantasy threats and the first glimpse of something older and stranger beneath the villain's operation.

#### THE LOW-TO-HIGH FANTASY ESCALATION LADDER (read this twice)

This is the single most delicate part of the whole crossover, because Brannok's campaign has been deliberately grounded and low-magic. If you turn the dial too fast, you break his story's tone and betray the request. Each rung of the ladder has a **physical, practical anchor**. The rule is never "the magic is mysterious." The rule is always "*this stone should be cold, and it's warm - why*"

**Rung 1 - Weird but explainable.**
A ward-stone overcharged with stolen ward-iron behaves wrong: a pressure field in the air, sound muffled, the smell of hot metal where there's no forge. No magic lecture. Just: someone tampered with these, and the tampering has side effects. Brannok can understand this as a craftsman.

**Rung 2 - Evidence Brannok can't verify himself.**
Zula's detection magic reveals invisible transit-mark signatures on crates, doors, and people; residue of suppression magic on a victim; the trace of a ritual in a building that looks like an ordinary inn. Brannok must *trust information he cannot confirm with his own senses.* This is his real challenge - not tactical, but a challenge to a man who believes only what he can see and weigh.

**Rung 3 - The first altered subject (body horror).**
At Survey Station Seven they find **Tessa Carn**, a former road-warden disappeared three years ago for trying to report falsified ward-iron. The Choir's memory-alteration took a specific form on her: she has been turned into a living filing system, unable to stop reciting route certifications in a voice that sounds like prayer. She is not dead. She is not free. This should disturb Brannok in a precise way - the corruption didn't just *use* a road, it turned *a person into a road*. This is the rung where Brannok feels the floor of his grounded world tilt.

**Rung 4 - Something older underneath.**
Below Station Seven is a chamber that predates the Survey by centuries. A map is carved into the stone floor - not a road map, a *ley-line* map - and the cipher on the routing documents matches its lines. The occupants have been using it as a ritual diagram without understanding what it is. When the party removes evidence from the building, the lines briefly *illuminate on their own*. Something notices. This is the first hint of the third faction (see Part Three) and the doorway into Act Three's full high fantasy.

> **The craftsman's tell.** Throughout Act Two, give Brannok small physical anomalies only he would catch: a ward-stone that cools when he touches it; Rook refusing to fly over one stretch of road for the first time ever; his hammer ringing true against a surface that should be too hard. He won't understand why. He's not supposed to yet. He has spent his whole life maintaining road wards. Something very old is starting to recognize him as a *warden* in a sense far deeper than a job title.

#### Key scenes

**Scene 4 - Survey Station Seven.**
A half-derelict coaching complex at an overgrown crossroads. The visible building is a wayhouse. The real facility is below, three levels in the old road-foundation stone:

- *Level 1:* cargo management, false manifests, ward-iron storage.
- *Level 2:* "processing" rooms where prisoners' names, papers, and identities are formally removed before transfer. The equipment is a horror-fusion of Choir ritual objects and Road Survey administrative seals - bureaucracy and cult, the same machine.
- *Level 3:* the old ley-line chamber.

**Scene 5 - The Field Captain, again.**
The Ashen Choir field captain who escaped Selene's finale commands the station. They are not the top villain - they answer to the Benefactor - but they're competent, dangerous, and personal: they know Selene's name and Brannok's face (from a Stonewake notice). The fight has environmental complications: the old stones surge unpredictably when violence happens near them.

**Scene 6 - Brannok and the thing he has no frame for.**
Tessa Carn recognizes Brannok's hammer - she was the warden who originally certified the very ward-iron components the Stonewake conspiracy later falsified. She tried to report it. She was disappeared. She has recited the correct certifications in a loop for three years. Brannok must decide whether she can be helped or is beyond practical rescue - his first "Kaelis situation" where the answer might be the hard one. Selene *can* partially restore her (not cleanly - this is not a tidy fix), and Brannok's road-warden vocabulary reaches her where magic alone can't (she responds to specific maintenance terminology). The choice of whether to attempt it, and what to do with her after, is a genuine moral decision with no free-lunch answer.

#### Act Two climax

The decoded cipher, Jasna's records work, and a sealed document from the processing room reveal the Benefactor's identity and location: **Orvaine Thresh**, a retired Senior Commissioner of the Continental Road Survey, currently at the Continental Survey Headquarters in the capital, scheduled to give testimony to a reformist road-safety inquiry in **four days**. If she testifies, she reclassifies the routing records under new administrative seals and the party's evidence becomes inadmissible through procedure. Four days to reach the capital, build the case, and stop the testimony.

And as they leave, the ley-line chamber *responds to the evidence being removed.* The lines light. Something old has been disturbed and is paying attention.

### ACT THREE - THE ROAD THAT REMEMBERS

**Fantasy dial: HIGH.** Full high fantasy, political and magical threat at once. The finale is a courtroom *and* a dungeon, running simultaneously.
**Tonal touchstone:** the grieving, beautiful, ancient-thing-asking-for-help register of *A Court of Fey & Flowers* at its most sincere, fused with the converging-tracks finale structure of *The Unsleeping City*.

#### The third faction - THE OLD WARDS

The ley-line map at Station Seven is the first proof of the **Original Survey**: a pre-civilization network of paths and way-markers laid down by a people so old they left no name. The modern Road Survey was built, unknowingly, on top of it. The ward-stones that keep roads safe don't merely tap ambient arcane energy - they tap the Original Survey's infrastructure, which is *semi-conscious, or at minimum responsive*.

Forty years of theft, falsification, and ritual abuse have wounded it. It's been trying to correct itself in small ways: improbable dead-ends, roads that grow inexplicably hard to travel, map anomalies no surveyor can explain. What the party felt as "the chamber responding" was the Original Survey recognizing allies.

**This is the rung that completes Brannok's arc.** He has spent his entire campaign repairing damaged road wards. In the eyes of something very old, he is a *warden* in the deepest possible sense. The low-fantasy dwarf who only believed what he could weigh becomes, by the end, a man an ancient intelligence chooses to help - *and he gets there honestly, one cooling stone at a time.*

#### The finale runs on two tracks

Split the party intelligently along capability lines. This is the Mulligan multi-track finale: everyone gets an equal stake, no single character carries the whole load.

**The Courtroom Track (Selene's strength).**
Selene, Marcella, Jasna, and Mirelle build and present the evidentiary case to the reformist inquiry, identify which inquiry members can be trusted, and counter Thresh's efforts to pre-discredit the evidence. The climactic inquiry session is a *social encounter with real stakes* - not combat. Two parties with irreconcilable narratives; one of them (Thresh) lying beautifully and calmly; the truth having to find a way through. This should be the best beat of a legal drama. It should be *hard*. Thresh is brilliant, experienced, and composed.

**The Underground Track (Brannok's strength).**
Brannok, Kara, Mara, and Needle handle the material track: retrieving physical evidence from a Survey Headquarters subbasement archive (legally where possible, otherwise not), locating and protecting surviving witnesses, and - at the climax - stopping Thresh's last contingency: a Choir ritual in the Headquarters' own ward-stone foundation, meant to permanently seal the routing cipher so the evidence becomes undecodable.

Those foundation ward-stones are part of the Original Survey. They know what's being done to them. They help Brannok the way old, tired things help someone who has spent a lifetime caring for them: showing him paths, dampening the Choir's ritual magic, letting his hammer ring true against stone that should be too hard. The final underground fight is in a *living space* - the stones shift and settle around the combat, and Brannok must win **without destroying the stones that are trying to help him.**

> **Rook and the familiars.** Rook can go where neither PC can follow and has absorbed enough of the cipher to flag significant documents by pattern. Ves and Zula float between tracks as problem-solvers when either plan hits a wall. Use the familiars to stitch the two tracks together visually and emotionally.

#### Key scenes

**Scene 7 - The Capital, Two Tracks.** Establish both tracks, let the players assign themselves, and run parallel pressure.

**Scene 8 - The Night Before.** The quiet scene Brennan always insists on before a finale. Brannok and Selene have a real conversation - not about their wounds, about their *ordinary selves*. What Brannok was like before the collapse. What Selene was doing before she became Selene Vaer. No mechanics. Entirely for the weight of what comes next. If a player doesn't want it, skip gracefully.

**Scene 9 - Thresh Moves First.** Thresh learns they're in the city and triggers contingencies: the evidence is pre-emptively (and falsely) classified as forgery via a forged internal notation; the key witness is moved under false papers; a Choir kill-team activates to eliminate the party before the inquiry opens. The final action sequence before the climax: protect the witness, recover the classified evidence by confronting the Survey bureaucracy directly, and neutralize the kill-team - all before dawn.

**Scene 10 - The Inquiry and the Ritual, simultaneous.** Cut between the two tracks. Selene faces Thresh across the inquiry floor. Brannok faces the last Choir lieutenant across the living foundation. The cuts should escalate together so the two climaxes land as one.

**Scene 11 - The Convergence.** Brannok protects the cipher keys from below; Selene presents the decoded evidence above. Thresh's composure finally breaks - not with drama, but *clinically*. She calculates her odds, finds them insufficient, and does the thing no one expects: she **confesses**. Not from remorse - from professional pride. She wants the record to show, correctly, that she built the system, ran it for forty years, and it worked perfectly until two extraordinarily inconvenient people started breaking it from opposite ends.

Her confession is also a weapon. She knows where twenty years of routing records are stored and which officials were complicit in which crimes. She offers the full archive in exchange for immunity.

**The final choice (no right answer - the Mulligan finale tax):** Take the deal and get a complete, clean record while Thresh walks free Or refuse, and risk never recovering everything without her cooperation There is no correct answer. The finale should cost something regardless.

#### Emotional resolution

- **For Brannok:** the road-ward system is whole. The Original Survey has been, in some small way, acknowledged. He did not wait too long this time. He did not wait at all.
- **For Selene:** the witness record is complete. The names of the disappeared are returned to the inquiry. They are not erased. She stood in front of people who wanted to make the evidence not count, and she made it count.
- **For the pair:** Brannok asks where Selene is going next. Selene asks what road Brannok is taking. Neither says *we should travel together.* But Rook lands on Ves's back, and Ves doesn't sting him, and both their riders notice.

## PART THREE - THE WORLD LAYER

### How the two settings connect geographically

Treat Brannok's region (Crowstep, Stonewake, the North Road) and Selene's region (Hillfort, Saint Orveth's Reliquary, the old hidden tunnels) as **two ends of the same continental trade-road network**, joined by a contested middle territory where Act One opens (Mirren's Crossing). The Continental Road Survey is the kingdom-wide institution that supposedly maintains all of it. Both campaigns' villains were *franchisees* of one continental operation.

### The three layers of "road magic," from grounded to mythic

1. **Practical road wards (Brannok's whole campaign):** stones that keep trade roads safe from raiders, beasts, and ambushes. Damaged wards = unsafe roads. Repaired wards = open roads. This stays true and concrete the entire time.
1. **Choir transit/erasure magic (Selene's whole campaign):** memory-damage, suppression, "quiet care" transfers, ward-stones repurposed to *trap and muffle* rather than protect. Already established; now revealed as riding on the practical ward network.
1. **The Original Survey (the new mythic layer):** the ancient, semi-conscious path-network underneath everything, wounded by forty years of abuse, quietly trying to heal. Introduced only in Act Two and paid off in Act Three.

Crucially: each layer is anchored to **physical objects** - stones, iron, ciphers, maps, brooches, tubes. Never let the mythic layer become an abstract lecture. It always shows up as *a thing in the room behaving wrong.*

### Keeping the contraband consistent

"Ward-iron" is the through-line physical contraband. It appears in Brannok's campaign (stolen ward iron, fake road-safety goods) and connects cleanly to the Choir's repurposed ward-stones in Selene's campaign. In the crossover it's revealed that the same redirected ward-iron supply feeds *both* the false road-safety market *and* the erasure-tunnel ward-stones. One supply chain. Two crimes.

## PART FOUR - THE TONE DOCUMENT

**Year One feels like:** *The Wire* meets *Tears of the Kingdom* - a grounded political crime drama that slowly reveals the world is held up by ancient infrastructure no one fully understands.

**Mid-arc feels like:** *The Name of the Wind* meets *Knives Out* - the heroes are craftspeople and investigators, not chosen ones, and the mystery satisfies because every reveal was clued from the start.

**Finale feels like:** *A Redtail's Dream* meets *Crown of Candy* - something old and grieving asks for help from the only people who know how to give it, and the cost of doing the right thing is real.

**What it never becomes:** an abstract puzzle game. Every piece of mystery is attached to a person. Every piece of corruption is attached to a victim. Every piece of ancient magic is attached to something you can touch. Brannok and Selene are not chosen ones and not prophesied. They are two people who were in the right places with the right skills and refused to stop. That is the whole of their qualification. It is enough.

-----

## PART FIVE - THE POST-FINALE SEED (only if they want more)

The Original Survey is conscious, or close to it, and has been wounded for forty years. Healing it isn't a matter of fixing stones - it's a matter of *traveling the original paths and re-establishing broken connections.* That's the seed of a much larger campaign: the Original Survey as a dungeon without walls, an ancient intelligence that needs warden-champions, and a world far more magical at its roots than anyone admitted. Plant nothing of this until the players signal they want to keep going.
