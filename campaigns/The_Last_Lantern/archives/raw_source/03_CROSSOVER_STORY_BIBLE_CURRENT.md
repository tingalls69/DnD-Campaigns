# The Last Lantern — Human-First Crossover

This rebuilt campaign keeps the emotional truth of Brannok Ashhelm and Selene Vaer but rebuilds the crossover around living people, visible danger, downtime that breathes, and choices that make the protagonists feel real.

The campaign is not driven by documents pointing to three roads. Evidence matters, but evidence is fuel, not the engine. The engine is this:

**Powerful people have spent decades deciding which communities, names, workers, witnesses, children, prisoners, and roads are acceptable losses. Brannok and Selene are the wrong two people to learn that this is still happening.**

The Last Lantern is now a campaign about refuge, rescue, protection, public courage, dangerous travel, hard humor, personal vows, and the cost of refusing to look away.

## Tone Target

Run this like a premium human-led actual-play fantasy campaign: emotionally sharp, funny without undercutting stakes, fast when danger hits, patient when characters need breath, full of callbacks, lived-in NPCs, and set pieces that people would want fan art of.

Use an energetic, cinematic human-DM feel. Do not copy or claim to be any real-world performer. The goal is not imitation. The goal is human-made specificity: voices with contradictions, jokes that become weapons, silence that lands, and fights with more objectives than damage.

## Core Promise

Every session should include at least one of these:
- Someone living needs protection.
- Someone says something that matters later.
- A joke reveals character.
- A meal, shop, inn, camp, wagon, or child makes the world feel worth saving.
- Brannok or Selene gets a real choice between anger, humor, tenderness, restraint, and violence.
- A fight changes the situation beyond HP loss.

# 12 Future Story Plans

DM-facing. Do not reveal future beats before play earns them.

## Rebuilt Campaign Acts

### Act I — The Hearthline Crossing
A refugee / witness caravan must cross dangerous roads while multiple factions try to seize people, destroy names, or collapse a route. Brannok and Selene meet through protection under pressure.

Key set pieces:
- Carter’s Rest refuge night;
- Daughters shopping / refit day before departure;
- children and Rook morale scene;
- first road breach with civilians present;
- Brannok and Selene forced to divide trust.

### Act II — The Town That Was Chosen to Fail
A whole settlement is being quietly sacrificed to preserve more profitable routes. The heroes must decide whether to expose the truth, evacuate, fight, or split forces.

Key set pieces:
- public square speech;
- ward-device with a living anchor;
- Mara holds a door;
- Zula disables magic under fire;
- Jasna preserves records while the building burns.

### Act III — The Acceptable Loss Engine
The party finds the living system behind Thresh’s choices: officials, old road hardware, compliant nobles, route agents, and terrified workers. The final conflict should force the villain’s moral claim into the open.

Key set pieces:
- dinner/hearing with a polite monster;
- massive multi-front fight using support wings;
- Rook/Ves rescue or morale beat;
- Brannok confronts the mine logic;
- Selene refuses clean erasure;
- the heroes choose what kind of road survives.

## Revision Permission
The story spine may change if the table finds a stronger emotional route. Keep the human stakes. Protect the characters. Let player words matter.

# 13 World and Faction Bible

## The Iron Road
Not just a road. A web of trade routes, ward-lines, old survey stations, relief houses, prisons, inns, wayhouses, markets, and communities that live or die by whether travel is safe.

## Road Wards
Practical fantasy infrastructure. They protect roads from monsters, raiders, beasts, ambushes, and route collapse. Damaged wards make roads unsafe. Repaired wards reopen life.

## Witness / Name Systems
Use as human stakes, not puzzle law. A stolen name means someone can be moved, hidden, renamed, denied, or erased from records and memory. The solution should involve rescue, testimony, public consequence, magic used plainly, and villains confronted.

## The Hearthline
A refugee and witness convoy. Soup, wagons, tired guards, children, livestock, broken wheels, evidence satchels, scared people, and heroes who need to sleep but will not.

## Thresh Network
A secular/institutional logistics network using old road authority, current corruption, and fear. Their evil is not that they worship a concept. Their evil is that they made spreadsheets out of suffering and called it civilization.

## Carter’s Rest
Emotional anchor. Refuge, meals, arguments, Rook’s crimes, Nera’s threats, exhausted laughter, and the place the campaign remembers why roads matter.
