Bridge Arc: “The Road of Quiet Names”

After Camp Highmore, the Daughters of Consequence did not leave immediately.

For three days, Camp Highmore remained open like a wound. The lower cells were emptied. The dumb bell chamber was mapped under witness. Filip’s former men were questioned in shifts. Shandra’s runners were separated, guarded, and cross-examined. Every name pulled from the drowned way had to be checked against ledgers, false release papers, bone tags, food allocations, burial claims, and missing-person reports.

Selene and the Daughters became the thing Camp Highmore needed and feared: outsiders with enough witnesses to make denial impossible.

Marcella turned the first tribunal session into a public humiliation. She did not shout. She did not need to. She made officers repeat their own contradictions aloud until the room began to understand the size of the lie.

Jasna lived in the seized records for two days, barely sleeping. She compared handwriting, seal pressure, ink age, copied names, and altered transfer marks. Her work proved that the lower-cell disappearances were not isolated abuses by Filip. They were part of a profit chain.

Kara and Eya spent most of that time with survivors. Ariane, Ren, Orren, Johan, Rell, Pella, and several nameless lower-cell prisoners were stabilized, fed, and protected. Kara stood watch herself when she decided a witness was too important to trust to embarrassed guards. Eya took testimony gently, sometimes using magic only when trauma made speech impossible.

Needle made herself useful in the ways Needle prefers: finding secondary doors, false bottoms, loose wall stones, and messenger routes no honest architect would admit existed. She also found three knives hidden in places where prisoners were supposed to be “secure.”

Zula examined the old route markings, the closed-eye tokens, the black wax slate, and the tube carried by Disposable. Her divinations confirmed something ugly: the Camp Highmore network was only a local knot. The thread ran south, then split.

One path led toward Ibrido.

Another led toward a smaller, quieter problem: a chain of missing villagers along the roads north and west, all near settlements whose food stores had begun to rot.

At first, it looked like residue from the same witness-erasure network. Names removed. Travelers gone. Families told that sons, daughters, workers, and debtors had simply moved on.

But the pattern was wrong.

The disappearances were too regular. Too ritualistic. One every third midnight. No ransom. No political pressure. No obvious profit.

The Daughters followed the lesser road first because it was immediate. A caravan out of Highmore had failed to arrive at a grain stop called Bellweather Ford. When the group found it, the horses were alive but half-starved, the wagon canvas was slashed from the inside, and the grain had blackened in its sacks.

That investigation became a short but dangerous road campaign.

They tracked an invisible attacker through wet fields by crushed grass, frightened animals, and the places where frost formed in unnatural spirals. Ves scented something dead but moving. Needle nearly caught the thing with a snare-wire strung between two broken fence posts. Kara’s radiant magic forced it into view for only a heartbeat: not a living kidnapper, but an undead servant wrapped in distortion and grave-cold air.

The trail led to three more sites:

The Miller’s Shrine, where old burial stones had been turned inward and a prayer bowl was filled with teeth.

The Ash Orchard, where Selene and Zula found necromantic residue clinging to blighted fruit.

The Shepherd’s Hollow, where Eya spoke with the dead long enough to learn the victims had been “tested,” not killed for revenge.

The Daughters saved five captives from a collapsed root cellar beneath the hollow. Two were dying. One had no memory of being taken. One kept repeating that “the dead man was looking for a house to wear.”

That phrase mattered.

It did not match C.’s style. It did not match Shandra’s pragmatism. It did not match House Tavaril’s cleanup work.

It pointed toward something older, hungrier, and less political.

By the end of the bridge arc, the Daughters had defeated several undead scouts, destroyed a minor necromantic holding site, rescued captives, recovered testimony from the dead, and identified a broader pattern of disappearances moving toward the town of Hillfort. That points directly into A False Life, where Hillfort is suffering disappearances every third midnight, blighted woods, rotting food, and the hidden work of Viktorai Darkstar.
